    Home Matters: How Family Background Shapes Student Success

    This page explores the powerful ways in which a student’s home environment influences their academic experience. By examining exam scores across varying levels of parental involvement and parental education, we highlight how support and educational background at home can shape academic outcomes. We also consider how physical distance from home affects students’ day-to-day engagement, showing that students who live farther away tend to attend class less consistently. Together, these visualizations connect family influence and accessibility to school with both performance and participation, offering a more complete picture of how factors beyond the classroom contribute to student success.

    Distance from Home, Distance from Class

    This graph focuses specifically on the relationship between how far students live from home and how regularly they attend class. By comparing average attendance levels across increasing distances, the visualization illustrates a clear pattern: as distance from home increases, consistent class attendance tends to decline. This suggests that logistical challenges—such as longer commutes, transportation limitations, or reduced daily support—may create barriers to regular attendance. Highlighting this relationship emphasizes that academic engagement is influenced not only by motivation or ability, but also by the practical realities of students’ home circumstances.

    Connection with family

    Students whose families live farther away from their respective school, and thus feel more responsible to keep in touch with their family, attend class less often. This might be due to them dealing with more homesickness, which would help to explain their greater amounts of interaction with their families.

    Lower Attendance Leads to Lower Exam Scores

    Less in-class attendance, more than any other factor we investigated, most negatively affects a students exam scores; students sacrifice a lot to keep in contact with their families.

    The Importance of a Small Difference

    Although these attendance rates only differ by 0.30-0.50, the size of this data set (n=6607) makes these results statistically significant at the a=0.05 level. In addition, this difference still affects their exams scores, and in a statistically significant way.

    Support at Home, Success at School

    This graph examines how students’ academic performance varies with levels of parental involvement and parental education. By comparing average exam scores across these categories, the visualization highlights a clear association between stronger support systems at home and higher academic achievement. Students with more involved parents or with parents who have higher levels of education tend to perform better on exams, suggesting that guidance, expectations, and access to educational resources at home play an important role in shaping outcomes. Together, these measures underscore how family engagement and educational background can meaningfully influence student success in the classroom.

    Parental Education Level

    Comparing across each overall group, parental education level significantly increases the scores that students from that group achieve on the exam, but less so than parental involvement differences within each group. Check here for an explanation of the statistical significance.

    Parental Level of Involvement

    Parental involvement, more than the factor of parental education level, more significantly impacts students' exam scores. Within each subgroup (each level of parental education), the level of parental involvement causes a stair-like ascendance from low to high levels of parental involvement, which positively impacts exam scores.

    Outliers

    Within the parental education groups, there are more outliers (dots above the box plots) for groups with lesser parental education. This reveals the stereotypical will to simply 'work harder' for students that might be more disadvantaged. In fact, the individual who scored the highest on this exam (exam_score = 101) was a part of the most disadvantaged group (parental level of high school, low parental motivation), which proves that the general trends we associate with the group aren't entirely true just for the individual.
